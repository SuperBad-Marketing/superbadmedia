04

Visual
Style

1
1970s Warmth
The brand draws on 1970s design — warm palettes, retro geometry, tactile imperfection. Not nostalgic for its own sake. The 70s reference is about warmth and personality over clinical modernism. Think: Brenton Wood album covers, vintage Penguin paperbacks, analogue warmth in a digital world.
2
Intentional Composition
Every frame is composed with intention — like a Wes Anderson shot. Symmetry where it creates authority. Asymmetry where it creates personality. Nothing is accidental. Generous negative space balanced against moments of controlled density. The composition should feel considered, even when the content is self-deprecating.
3
High Production / Low Ego
The visual identity is polished and cinematic. The content inside it is self-deprecating and human. The contrast between the two is intentional and is the brand's defining characteristic. Think: beautifully shot footage of someone looking mildly embarrassed. That tension is the whole thing.
4
Dark Over Light
Default is dark — Dark Charcoal (#1A1A18) as the primary environment. Light-on-dark creates drama and presence without trying. When light backgrounds are required (print, formal documents), use Warm Cream (#FDF5E6) never pure white. Pure white feels cold and corporate. That's not this.
5
Cinematic Framing
All photography and video should feel like it belongs in a film, not a stock library. Candid over posed. Real emotion over manufactured expression. The armchair set — red backdrop, warm lighting, retro chair — is the visual anchor. All content should feel like it could exist in that same universe.
6
Typography as Visual Element
Headlines are visual elements as much as text. Large Black Han Sans type at display sizes — bleeding off edges, overlapping imagery, used as graphic shapes. The SUPERBAD logo fills frames. Text is never just text. It's part of the composition. Reference: the teaser video where SUPERBAD fills the screen over the face.
05

Visual
References

🎬
Wes Anderson
Primary Visual Reference
Intentional symmetry. Warm palettes. Absurd premises treated with complete visual sincerity. Fourth wall acknowledged casually. Every frame composed as if it matters — even when the subject doesn't take itself seriously. This is the visual target.
📼
1970s Design
Era Reference
Warm earth tones, retro typography, analogue texture. Not retro for its own sake — for the warmth and personality it carries. Vinyl album artwork. Penguin book covers. Harvest gold and burnt sienna. The aesthetic that says: made by a human, not a committee.
🎞
Editorial Photography
Photography Direction
Candid, warm, cinematic. Never stock. Never posed. The best frames capture emotion without staging it — a laugh caught mid-sentence, a genuine reaction, a moment of focus. All photography should feel like it belongs in a documentary about people who are good at what they do.
🎭
The Armchair Set
Brand Environment
SuperBad Red backdrop. Burnt yellow retro armchair. Warm practical lighting. This is the brand's home environment — the visual anchor of the Armchair Sessions and the spatial identity of the brand. All content should feel like it could exist in this world.
06

Visual
Do / Don't

✕ DON'T
✕Use pure black (#000000) or pure white (#FFFFFF) — they feel cold and corporate. Always use Dark Charcoal or Warm Cream instead.
✕Use stock photography. SuperBad content is always real, always shot by SuperBad, always specific to the client or moment.
✕Use more than two typefaces in any single piece of content. The hierarchy is defined — respect it.
✕Use gradients that don't reference the brand palette. No purple, no blue, no "modern tech" colour schemes.
✕Use the Pacifico script font for anything other than the logo. It exists for one purpose.
✕Create content that looks like every other marketing agency. If it could have been made by anyone, it shouldn't be made by SuperBad.
✓ DO
✓Default to dark backgrounds with Warm Cream text and SuperBad Red accents. This is the home environment.
✓Use Black Han Sans at display sizes — big, bold, composited over imagery. Let the type be a visual element.
✓Shoot everything as if it belongs in a film. Candid, warm, real. The camera catches people being themselves — not performing for it.
✓Compose with intention. Every element placed deliberately. Generous white space balanced with moments of density.
✓Use Playfair Display italic for quotes and the manifesto. The serif creates intentional contrast against the sans-serif system.
✓Make it recognisably SuperBad. Someone should see it without a logo and know exactly who made it.
Motion Direction
Cuts are clean and confident — no cheap transitions, no wipes, no fades unless they serve the narrative. Hard cuts between armchair chunks. B-roll transitions with purpose. The dramatic music swell followed by a dead stop is the brand's signature motion moment — used sparingly because it only works when it's unexpected.
Hard cuts Dead stops Purposeful B-roll No cheap transitions No wipes or fades
Overall Feeling
Someone stumbling across SuperBad content should feel like they've found something — not been targeted by something. The visual identity is confident without being loud. Premium without being cold. Funny without announcing it. The brand looks like it has something to say and doesn't particularly care whether you listen — which, paradoxically, makes you want to listen.
