Claude's pick

A · Warm Filmic

Portra 400, on a good day.

Warm highlights, gently lifted blacks, faithful skin tones, restrained saturation. The standard premium-commercial grade of the last five years — Apple product films, A24 trailers, Aesop campaigns, every wedding photographer whose Instagram you secretly admire.

Reads as 2026. Carries the warmth the brand asks for without announcing an era. The retro soul comes from composition, subject, and pacing — not from the grade shouting "70s!"

Risk Ubiquitous — every premium brand uses some version of this grade right now.

B · 70s Warm Fade

Kodachrome, aged in a drawer.

Heavy lifted blacks to warm brown, strong yellow/orange cast in highlights, muted saturation, low contrast. Explicitly referencing 1970s film stocks — Kodachrome and Ektachrome, faded by time.

Most on-brief for "1970s warmth" in the brand rules, but period-coded: the grade itself announces retro, rather than the soul being retro in a modern grade. Most likely of the four to look tired by 2030.

Risk Period pastiche. Announces the era instead of inhabiting it.

C · Warm-neutral Cinematic

Less faded, more confident.

Warm skin and highlights, but blacks held near true black and contrast held high. Closer to modern cinema (Deakins, Fraser) than to modern commercial photography. Genre-neutral and era-proof.

Retro soul has to come entirely from composition and subject — the grade itself is timeless rather than era-specific. Potentially the longest-lived of the four, but cooler in feel than the brand sells.

Risk Grade feels slightly cooler than SuperBad's warm brand.

D · Dual Grade

Two registers. One agency.

Creative work (brand films, lifestyle, portraits) gets the warm filmic grade (essentially A). Performance-adjacent imagery (case studies, Services stills, data-backed work) gets a flatter, more neutral, documentary grade — less styled, more "this is what actually happened".

Honest about SuperBad's two service lines. Overbuilt for v1 (performance imagery is minimal on launch) and means visitors see two visual registers on one site, which can dilute the signature.

Risk Two grades = no signature grade. And v1 barely has performance imagery anyway.

How to read this

Skin tones are the first place any grade wins or loses. Check whether portraits still feel like skin (not jaundiced, not green, not dead). Then check whether product shots still sell — a grade that kills colour saturation too far makes food look stale and clothing look faded. Then lifestyle — whether the scene still feels like the time of day it was actually shot.
